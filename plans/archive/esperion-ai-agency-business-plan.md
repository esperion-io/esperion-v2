## 4. Pricing Model Analysis: Esperion V1 vs. Chris Koerner — Recommendation

This is a critical strategic decision. Both models work. The question is which generates better revenue, scales better, and is easier to sell in the NZ SMB market.

---

### Model A: Esperion V1 (Original)
*High setup fee + fixed monthly retainer*

| Tier | Setup Fee | Monthly Retainer |
|---|---|---|
| Tier 1 | $1,500–$2,500 | $500–$800 |
| Tier 2 | $3,000–$5,000 | $1,000–$1,500 |
| Tier 3 | $5,000–$10,000 | $2,000–$3,000 |

**Example (Tier 1):** $2,000 setup + $650/month = $9,800 in Year 1

#### Pros
- High setup revenue = immediate cash flow
- Monthly is decoupled from setup cost — can be set higher than 20%
- Good for early cash flow while client base is small
- Standard agency model; clients are familiar with it

#### Cons
- High setup fee is a significant barrier to close (requires budget approval)
- Monthly rate feels arbitrary — why $650 and not $800? Hard to justify
- Doesn't scale well as explanation for renewal ("what am I paying for?")
- Prospect negotiates setup down, monthly gets squeezed too

---

### Model B: Chris Koerner's Model
*Lower/mid setup + 20% of setup fee monthly*

| Setup Complexity | Setup Fee | Monthly (20%) |
|---|---|---|
| Basic | $500–$1,500 | $100–$300 |
| Standard | $2,000–$3,000 | $400–$600 |
| Complex | $5,000+ | $1,000+ |

**Example (Standard):** $2,000 setup → $400/month = $6,800 in Year 1

#### Pros
- Lower setup = dramatically lower barrier to entry (easier to close)
- Monthly fee is **self-justifying**: "You paid $2K to build this — $400/month is 20% to keep it running, updated, and improved. Makes sense."
- Scales logically with complexity (more complex = higher setup = higher monthly)
- Prospect understands the formula; less negotiation friction
- Anchors monthly value to what was built, not an arbitrary number

#### Cons
- Lower setup revenue = more cash flow pressure in months 1–3
- At 20%, low setups generate very low MRR (e.g., $500 setup → $100/month is too thin)
- Doesn't capture full market rate for monthly if setup was priced lean

---

### Head-to-Head Comparison

| Dimension | Esperion V1 | Koerner | Winner |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ease of sale (upfront) | Harder (bigger check) | Easier (lower setup) | **Koerner** |
| Year 1 revenue per client (mid setup) | $9,800 | $6,800 | **V1** |
| Year 1 revenue per client (higher setup) | $14,200 | $14,000 | Tie |
| Monthly justifiability | Low (feels arbitrary) | High (formula-based) | **Koerner** |
| Cash flow months 1–3 | Strong | Weaker | **V1** |
| Scales with complexity | Partially | Naturally | **Koerner** |
| Client retention argument | Weak | Strong ("20% to maintain what you built") | **Koerner** |
| Total Y1 revenue at 40 clients | $659K | ~$530K | **V1** |
| Total Y2 MRR at 96 clients | $95K+ | ~$78K+ | **V1** |
| Scalability (beyond 50 clients) | Medium | High | **Koerner** |

---

### ✅ RECOMMENDATION: Hybrid Model (Best of Both)

Don't choose one. Build a hybrid that uses the **logic of Koerner** (formula-based monthly) with **pricing floors and ceilings** that protect revenue and reflect NZ market rates.

**The Hybrid Formula:**
- Setup fee reflects actual complexity (not capped at Koerner's low end)
- Monthly = **25% of setup fee**, with a hard floor of $500/month
- At higher setup tiers, monthly is capped at a ceiling to stay competitive

**Why 25% instead of 20%:**
- Koerner's 20% was modelled on US market pricing (USD)
- NZ market can support 25% because competition is minimal
- At 25%, even a $2,000 setup generates $500/month — a sustainable floor
- The formula is still simple and self-justifying

### Hybrid Pricing Table

| Tier | Setup Fee (NZD) | Monthly (25% of setup, floor $500) | Year 1 Per Client | Example Client |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Tier 1 | $2,000–$3,000 | $500–$750 | $8,000–$12,000 | Plumber, dentist |
| Tier 2 | $4,000–$6,500 | $1,000–$1,625 | $16,000–$26,000 | Property manager, RE agency |
| Tier 3 | $8,000–$14,000 | $2,000–$3,500 | $32,000–$56,000 | Recruitment firm, law firm |

**The sales script for monthly pricing:**
> "We charge 25% of the setup cost per month to keep it running, maintained, improved, and supported. You paid $4,000 to build the system — $1,000/month keeps it in top shape and means we're always improving it. That's our deal."

This is clean, simple, and easy to defend. It holds up in negotiations because the logic is airtight.

## 13. Risks & Mitigations

### Risk 1: AI Tools Change Fast (HIGH probability, MEDIUM impact)

**The risk:** Vapi pivots, pricing changes, a core tool shuts down or commoditises.  
**Mitigation:** Never be dependent on a single tool. Build on Make.com and OpenClaw as the orchestration layer. Maintain 2–3 options for each function. OpenClaw itself is our primary moat — and we have power-user knowledge that isn't easy to replicate.

### Risk 2: Platform Vendors Build Native AI (HIGH probability, MEDIUM-HIGH impact)

**The risk:** Cliniko, PropertyMe, ServiceM8 add decent AI features natively.  
**Mitigation:** Takes them 2–3 years to do it well. By then we're entrenched. We'll also integrate on top of these platforms, not compete. As basic AI gets commoditised, we move upmarket to more complex use cases — custom OpenClaw PAs being the clearest example.

### Risk 3: Anthony's Time Bandwidth (MEDIUM probability, HIGH impact)

**The risk:** Anthony is running 4 other businesses. This demands real sales effort in months 1–6.  
**Mitigation:** This venture has the highest revenue ceiling of any current operation. 90-day focused sprint. First hire at Month 8 removes delivery burden. Clear calendar blocks for sales calls.

### Risk 4: Client Churn if Results Don't Show (LOW probability if executed well, HIGH impact)

**The risk:** Client signs up, AI underperforms, cancels after 3 months.  
**Mitigation:** Only take clients where ROI is clear and measurable. Set expectations in writing before signing. Track metrics from Day 1 (calls handled, leads captured, hours saved, revenue recovered). Make the value visible every month. Founding clients: go above and beyond. Their testimonials are everything.

### Risk 5: Regulatory (Privacy Act, Health Data) (LOW-MEDIUM probability, MEDIUM impact)

**The risk:** Medical/dental client experiences a data exposure through an AI system we built.  
**Mitigation:** Never store patient data ourselves. All data stays in client's existing systems (Cliniko, Best Practice). Get T&Cs reviewed by a lawyer. Add privacy compliance review to medical delivery checklist. Health vertical: mandatory compliance sign-off before go-live.

### Risk 6: Competition Intensifies (MEDIUM probability, 12–18 months)

**The risk:** A well-funded AU agency or pivoting NZ digital agency enters the space.  
**Mitigation:** Build moat through vertical expertise, client relationships, and OpenClaw configuration depth. Be so embedded in the client's operations that switching costs are painful — both technically and relationally. Service quality over price.

### Risk 7: OpenClaw Pricing or Access Changes (LOW probability, MEDIUM impact)

**The risk:** OpenClaw pricing changes significantly or enterprise licensing becomes required.  
**Mitigation:** This is our own stack — we have advance knowledge of changes. Also: OpenClaw is one layer of a multi-tool stack. We can substitute or supplement with other PA-layer tools if required. Client relationships don't depend on one tool.

### Risk 8: NZ Market Too Small for Year 3 Projections (LOW probability, LOW-MEDIUM impact)

**The risk:** We run out of addressable clients in NZ verticals.  
**Mitigation:** NZ trades alone = 50,000+ businesses. 150 clients = 0.3% market share. Not a ceiling problem. AU expansion is natural by Year 3 — same verticals, same tools, bigger market.
